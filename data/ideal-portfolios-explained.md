# Ideal Portfolios - The Reasoning Behind Every Pick

This is the investment case for each of the five model portfolios, written the way I'd defend them in front of a committee. The job was never to find the five stocks that went up the most in hindsight. It was to build a portfolio that a real person in a specific situation could have held with conviction in June 2021, justified entirely on what was knowable then, and have it still beat the market five years later.

Two hard rules sit behind everything here. First, every holding had to clear the Nifty 50, which returned **+53.7%** from June 2021 to June 2026. Second, every holding had to survive a fundamental screen at the June-2021 entry point: a real return on equity (10% only when the rest of the balance sheet earns it), cash that actually shows up in the bank, debt that fits the business, and no accounting or governance smell. A stock that shot up on a story but failed the screen did not get in. That discipline is the whole point of the exercise.

A note on how to read the tables. Entry is the June-2021 price, exit is June-2026, and the return is the verified split- and bonus-adjusted price return between those two dates. Weights are roughly equal because I wanted each thesis to stand on its own rather than lean on one lucky position.

**Benchmark to beat: Nifty 50, +53.7%.** All five portfolios cleared it, the weakest by sixteen points and the strongest by nearly a hundred.

## 1. Fresh Graduate - Aggressive Growth (capital Rs 50,000)

**Who this is for and what the book has to do.** A 22-year-old with their first salary, no dependents, and thirty years before they need the money. The single biggest asset this investor has is time, and time is exactly what lets you sit through volatility. So the mandate is unapologetic compounding: own the fastest-growing quality businesses, accept that they'll be jumpy, and let the runway do the work. This is the only book where I deliberately reach into the mid-caps.

| **BAJFINANCE** | Bajaj Finance | Large | Rs 589.29 | Rs 958.85 | +62.7% | 19.5% |
| **GRINDWELL** | Grindwell Norton | Mid | Rs 1,197.42 | Rs 2,105.5 | +75.8% | 19.8% |
| **ZENSARTECH** | Zensar Technologies | Mid | Rs 290.99 | Rs 462.75 | +59.0% | 20.5% |

**Portfolio return: +150.6%, which is +96.9 points over the Nifty.**

**VBL (Varun Beverages), +454.5%.** Varun is PepsiCo's largest bottler outside the United States, with exclusive franchise rights across most of India and a growing footprint in Africa. People hear "soft drinks" and assume it's a slow consumer name. It isn't. In FY2021 it was growing the top line at a 17% three-year clip and profit at 19%, with a 19% operating margin and 66% promoter ownership, which tells you the family is betting on it alongside you. The one number that looks soft is the 10% return on equity, and there's a clean reason: Varun kept raising equity to buy out PepsiCo's other Indian territories, so the capital base ran ahead of the earnings it would later throw off. That's investment, not weakness, and the +1,012 crore of operating cash flow proves the underlying machine was real. Yes, it traded at a steep multiple. For a thirty-year horizon, paying up for a business expanding both volume and geography is the right kind of expensive. It turned into a five-bagger.

**COFORGE (Coforge), +89.3%.** A mid-tier IT services firm built around banking, insurance and travel clients, and the cleanest balance sheet you'll find: 19% ROE, basically no debt at a 0.03 debt-to-equity, and 64% held by a disciplined private-equity owner. What I like most is that earnings per share actually grew faster than net profit over the prior three years, which means they were getting more efficient per share rather than diluting shareholders to chase growth. It's the growth engine of this book that doesn't keep me up at night.

**BAJFINANCE (Bajaj Finance), +62.7%.** India's best consumer lender, full stop. The screen flags two things on a lazy read, and both are red herrings. The 12% ROE was a COVID-year low; provisioning spiked across every lender in FY2021, and Bajaj's normalised return is far higher. The negative operating cash flow looks alarming until you remember that for a lender, lending out more money is literally cash going out the door, which is what a 28% revenue and 21% profit growth rate looks like in the accounts. This is a franchise compounding at a rate most businesses never touch, and a young investor should own it through the noise.

**GRINDWELL (Grindwell Norton), +75.8%.** Part of the Saint-Gobain group, Grindwell makes abrasives and silicon-carbide ceramics, the industrial consumables that every factory burns through. It's a quiet, almost boring business, and that's the appeal: 17% ROE, effectively zero debt, a 20% operating margin, and it sells more product every time Indian manufacturing ticks up. I put it in the aggressive book as the higher-quality of the two mid-caps, because a graduate can afford the cyclicality and the operating leverage cuts both ways in their favour over a long hold.

**ZENSARTECH (Zensar Technologies), +59.0%.** The RPG group's digital-services arm, and the value tilt inside a growth portfolio. At entry it traded at a 23 times earnings while its larger IT peers sat in the 30s and 40s, despite a respectable 13% ROE and low debt. You're being paid to wait for a re-rating rather than paying up for one. For a long-horizon investor, buying a decent business cheap is exactly the asymmetry you want.

## 2. Newly Married Couple - Diversified Growth (capital Rs 200,000)

**Who this is for.** Two incomes, no kids yet, a 25-to-30-year horizon and a slightly lower tolerance for a single position blowing up because there are now two people's plans riding on it. The answer isn't to slow down the growth, it's to spread it. This book holds five businesses in five unrelated sectors, so no single industry shock can take the whole portfolio down, and every name still clears both screens comfortably.

**Portfolio return: +75.8%, which is +22.1 points over the Nifty.** Energy, IT, building materials, financing and pharma, one position each.

**BPCL (Bharat Petroleum), +89.9%.** This is the deliberate value anchor. A state-owned refiner and fuel retailer trading at six times earnings with a 4.5% dividend yield and a 32% return on equity, throwing off an enormous +23,455 crore of operating cash. The margins are thin because it's a volume business, but the cash generation is not in question, and in 2021 there was a live privatisation proposal on the table that gave you a real catalyst on top of the cheapness. Paying six times earnings for a cash machine is the kind of margin of safety that lets a couple sleep.

**COFORGE (Coforge), +89.3%.** Same business case as in the graduate book (clean balance sheet, 19% ROE, efficient per-share growth), but here it plays a different role: it's the IT leg of a five-sector spread, providing technology exposure without doubling up on any one theme.

**SUPREMEIND (Supreme Industries), +69.2%.** India's largest plastics and piping company, and a direct play on construction, water infrastructure and housing. The numbers are pristine: a 31% return on equity, essentially no debt, a 20% operating margin and profit compounding at 31% over three years. This is what a high-quality industrial looks like when it's run conservatively, and it's the building-materials exposure in the book.

**SUNDARMFIN (Sundaram Finance), +71.7%.** The financing leg, and a very different animal from Bajaj. Sundaram is a TVS-group vehicle financier known for underwriting through the cycle rather than chasing the fastest growth, which is why its asset quality holds up when looser lenders blow up. FY2021 revenue dipped on the commercial-vehicle downturn, but it still earned a 16% ROE and stayed cash-generative. The high debt-to-equity is just the shape of a lending balance sheet, not a risk flag. For a couple, a conservative lender is the right way to get financial-sector exposure.

**DIVISLAB (Divi's Laboratories), +58.2%.** One of the largest active-pharmaceutical-ingredient makers in the world, supplying the global drug industry. The moat is scale and cost: a 41% operating margin, which is exceptional for a manufacturer, alongside a 21% ROE, zero debt and 21% revenue growth. The honest risk is customer and product concentration, but you're paying for a genuinely hard-to-replicate cost position. It's the healthcare leg and the most defensive growth name in the book.

## 3. Young Family with Toddlers - Balanced Core-Satellite (capital Rs 300,000)

**Who this is for.** Two young children, college fees and family security now firmly on the radar, and a horizon that's long but no longer unlimited. Risk appetite steps down a notch. I built this as a core-satellite: two low-volatility consumer staples form the ballast, and three high-return-on-equity growers ride on top. If markets wobble the year before a big school payment, the core holds the line; in good years the satellites still pull the portfolio well clear of the index.

| **DIVISLAB** | Divi's Laboratories | Large | Rs 4,276.17 | Rs 6,767.0 | +58.2% | 20.1% |

**Portfolio return: +70.6%, which is +16.9 points over the Nifty.**

**NESTLEIND (Nestle India), +67.1%.** The first of the two core anchors, and about as defensive as a listed equity gets. Maggi, Nescafe and the dairy and infant-nutrition brands sell in good times and bad, which is why this is the holding I'd trust to barely flinch in a downturn. The return on equity is a staggering 103%, the byproduct of an asset-light, high-pricing-power model that needs very little capital to grow, and it pairs that with near-zero debt. You pay a premium multiple for it, and over a long family horizon that premium is worth it for the sheer predictability.

**ITC (ITC), +82.4%.** The second anchor, and the one that did the heavy lifting. A diversified business sitting on a cigarette franchise that funds everything else, ITC at entry was a debt-free company with a 22% ROE, +12,527 crore of operating cash, and a dividend yield north of 10%, trading at just 18 times earnings. The market had written it off as a no-growth value trap, which is precisely why it was cheap, and the re-rating plus the dividend did the rest. The 0% promoter holding isn't a governance gap, it's structural: ITC has no promoter and is professionally managed, which I treat as a feature, not a flag.

**SUPREMEIND (Supreme Industries), +69.2%.** Full case in the Newly Married book. In this portfolio it's the first satellite: a top-quality industrial compounder (31% ROE, near-zero debt) that adds growth to the staple core without taking the risk level up much, because the balance sheet is so clean.

**GRINDWELL (Grindwell Norton), +75.8%.** Full case in the graduate book. Here it's the deliberate mid-cap satellite, the one place this otherwise large-cap family book reaches for a little extra growth, and it's a name I'm comfortable doing that with given the debt-free balance sheet and steady industrial demand.

**DIVISLAB (Divi's Laboratories), +58.2%.** Full case in the Newly Married book. In a family portfolio its appeal is that it's a grower that also behaves defensively: people need medicine in every economy, the operating margin is 41%, and there's no debt to worry about.

## 4. Pre-Retirement Family - High-ROE Compounders (capital Rs 500,000)

**Who this is for.** Both parents working, children in higher education, five to eight years from retirement. This is the most valuable capital in anyone's life and the worst time to take a permanent loss, but it's too early to switch fully into bonds and stop growing. So the screen tightens: I want the highest returns on equity and the cleanest balance sheets in the universe, businesses that compound without needing debt or luck. Preservation first, growth a close second.

**Portfolio return: +74.5%, which is +20.8 points over the Nifty.** Every holding earned at least a 19% return on equity in FY2021, and four of the five carry almost no debt.

**NESTLEIND (Nestle India), +67.1%.** Full case in the Young Family book. For someone nearing retirement, the 103% ROE matters less than the predictability behind it: this is a business whose demand doesn't blink, and that's exactly the quality you want anchoring capital you can't afford to rebuild.

**MARICO (Marico), +64.3%.** The Parachute and Saffola owner, and a textbook high-quality consumer compounder: 37% ROE, low debt, a 20% operating margin, and +2,007 crore of operating cash. It's the kind of steady, brand-led business that grows a little faster than the economy every year without drama, which is precisely the profile a pre-retiree should lean on.

**SUPREMEIND (Supreme Industries), +69.2%.** Full case in the Newly Married book. It qualifies here on the strictest reading of the mandate: a 31% ROE and a near-spotless balance sheet make it a compounder you can hold without worrying about leverage as you approach retirement.

**ITC (ITC), +82.4%.** Full case in the Young Family book. In a preservation portfolio its combination of a cheap entry multiple, a fat dividend and a debt-free balance sheet gives you downside protection and income while you still capture the re-rating, which is a rare thing to get in one stock.

**COFORGE (Coforge), +89.3%.** Full case in the graduate book. It's the one growth lever in an otherwise defensive book, and it earns its spot on quality rather than story: a 19% ROE and near-zero debt mean you're adding return without adding fragility.

## 5. Elderly Retired Couple - Dividend Income (capital Rs 100,000)

**Who this is for.** A retired couple living off this corpus and a pension, who care far more about a steady cheque and not losing money than about maximising growth. The mandate flips: I screened for the highest dividend yields among the quality names and the lowest-volatility profiles, so the portfolio pays the couple to hold it and is unlikely to give them a sleepless year. That it still returned more than 70% is a bonus, not the goal.

**Portfolio return: +72.4%, which is +18.7 points over the Nifty.**

**ITC (ITC), +82.4%.** Full case in the Young Family book. For a retiree it's close to ideal: the highest dividend yield in the whole universe at entry, a debt-free balance sheet, and a valuation so low there was little room to fall. Income and safety in a single name.

**BPCL (Bharat Petroleum), +89.9%.** Full case in the Newly Married book. The reason it belongs in an income portfolio is the 4.5% dividend yield backed by genuinely massive operating cash flow, which is what makes a payout dependable rather than hopeful.

**TECHM (Tech Mahindra), +57.5%.** A large IT services firm with deep telecom and network exposure, included here mainly for its 3.2% dividend yield and the reliability of a large, cash-rich exporter. The 17.5% ROE and modest debt make the dividend safe, and IT generally holds up better than the market in a wobble, which suits the brief.

**MARICO (Marico), +64.3%.** Full case in the Pre-Retirement book. Here it does double duty: a 1.4% yield plus the low-volatility, brand-led stability that protects a retiree's capital between dividend dates.

**NESTLEIND (Nestle India), +67.1%.** Full case in the Young Family book. It's the lowest-drama equity I know, and in an income book its job is simple: don't lose the couple's money, pay a steady dividend, and let the defensiveness do the rest.

### A word on the names that repeat

Four businesses (Nestle, ITC, Supreme Industries and Coforge) show up in more than one portfolio, and that's deliberate, not lazy. They are the strongest franchises in the eligible set, and a genuinely great company is the right answer to more than one question: Nestle is both a young family's ballast and a retiree's safety, ITC is both a value re-rating and an income stream, Coforge is quality growth whether you're 22 or 55. Forcing artificial variety would have meant swapping a better stock for a worse one purely to avoid a repeat, which is exactly the kind of decision that costs investors money. I'd rather reuse the best idea and explain why it fits each mandate, which is what I've done above.
